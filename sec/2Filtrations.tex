\section{Filtrations}
All rings will be commutative with an identity element. 
\begin{defi}
    Given a ring $(A,+,\cdot)$ and a topology on $A$, we say that $A$ is a \emph{topological ring} if $(A,+)$ is a topological group and $\cdot\colon A\times A\to A$ is continuous.
\end{defi}

We will study topologies generated by an ideal of a ring $A$,

\begin{defi}
    Let $A$ be a ring and $\fraka\leq A$ and ideal. We will call \emph{$\fraka$-adic topology} on $A$ the topology given by the fundamental system of neighborhoods of $0$ $(\fraka^n)_{n\geq 0}$.
\end{defi}

\begin{prop}
    $A$ with the $\fraka$-adic topology is a topological ring.
\end{prop}
\begin{proof}
    We only need to check that $\cdot$ is continuous. We can reduce it to open sets of the form $ab+\fraka^n$ since $(A,+)$ is a topological abelian group. By the definition of an ideal we have 
    \[
    (a+\fraka^n)(b+\fraka^n)\subseteq ab+\fraka^n
    \]
    so $\cdot$ is continuous.
\end{proof}
 
We know that the product of two Cauchy sequences is Cauchy and the product of a Cauchy sequence by a sequence that converges to $0$ converges to $0$. Therefore $A^\mathbb N_\mathcal C$ is a ring and $[(0)_{n\geq 0}]$ is an ideal and so $\hat A$ is a ring. Since the ideals $(\widehat{\fraka^n})_{n\geq 0}$ are a fundamental systems of neighborhoods of $0$ we can view $\hat A$ as a topological ring. However it is not always true that $\widehat{\fraka^n}=\hat\fraka^n$ so the initial topology on $\hat A$ may not be its $\hat\fraka$-topology. In particular, $\hat A$ may not be complete with respect to its $\hat\fraka$-topology. 

\begin{defi}
    Let $M$ be an $A$-module such that $M$ is a topological space. We say that $M$ is a \emph{topological $A$-module} if $(M,+)$ is a topological abelian group and $\cdot\colon A\times M\to M$ is continuous.
\end{defi}

Likewise, we can check that the topology generated by the decreasing sequence of subgroups $(\fraka^nM)_{n\geq 0}$ gives $M$ the structure of a topological $A$-module. Since $M/\fraka^nM$ is an $A/\fraka^n$-module and there is a canonical projection $\theta_{n+1}\colon A/\fraka^{n+1}\twoheadrightarrow A/\fraka^n$, we have a canonical homomorphism of modules
\[
\alpha_{n+1}\colon M/\fraka^{n+1}M \twoheadrightarrow M/\fraka^nM
\]
i.e., $\alpha_{n+1}(\lambda a)=\theta_{n+1}(\lambda)\alpha_{n+1}(a)$ where $\lambda\in A/\fraka^{n+1}$ and $a\in M/\fraka^{n+1}M$. With this in mind the completion $\hat M$ becomes an $\hat A$-module which is, in fact, a topological $\hat A$-module.

We wish to compare other topologies of $M$ generated by a decreasing chain of submodules. We will need the following definition;


\begin{defi}
    Let $M$ be an $A$-module. A decreasing sequence of submodules
    \[
    M=M_0\supseteq M_1\supseteq \cdots\supseteq M_n\supseteq \cdots
    \]
    is called a \emph{filtration} of $M$. 
    
    Let $\fraka$ be an ideal of $A$. A filtration $(M_n)_{n\geq 0}$ of $M$ is called an \emph{$\fraka$-filtration} if $\fraka M_n\subseteq M_{n+1}$ for all $n\geq 0$, and a \emph{stable $\fraka$-filtration} if there exists $n_0\in\mathbb N$ such that the equality holds for all $n\geq n_0$. 
\end{defi}

We will see that all stable $\fraka$-filtrations define the same topology on $M$:
\begin{lem}
    If $(M_n)_{n\geq 0}$ is a stable $\fraka$-filtration of $M$ there exists an integer $n_0\in\mathbb N$ such that $M_{n+n_0}\subseteq \fraka^nM$ and $\fraka^{n+n_0}M\subseteq M_n$ for all $n\geq 0$. We call the topology generated by any stable $\fraka$-filtration of $M$ the \emph{$\fraka$-topology}.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
    Since $\fraka M_n\subseteq M_{n+1}$ we have $\fraka^nM\subseteq M_{n+1}$. Let $n_0\in \mathbb N$ be such that $\fraka M_n=M_{n+1}$ for all $n\geq n_0$, hence $M_{n+n_0}=\fraka^n M_{n_0}\subseteq \fraka^n M$ for all $n\geq 0$.
\end{proof}
